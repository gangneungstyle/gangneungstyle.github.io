Korean housing

23 Sep 2014 by Narelle

On the weekend we moved into our own home - no more hotels for a while! The apartments come with nothing, but we now have a fridge/freezer, microwave and washing machine, so we can live there. While we are waiting for the sofa bed to arrive, we are camping out Korean style (mattress on the floor) which is actually better than we expected.

Everything in the apartment is electronic which is nice, but sometimes a little difficult to work things out when they’re all in Korean! It took me half an hour to work out how to turn on the lights, and in 24 hours we haven’t managed to get hot water yet. Other things we can use the panel for include video calling the other apartments in the complex or checking the time and photo of anyone who rang the doorbell!

Most people (like us) who grew up in the suburbs with backyards to play in would probably be horrified at the idea of living in a big block of high rise apartments. But to the contrary, I’m really growing to like the idea. It means shared resources such as a gym, indoor golf, a library and several kids’ playgrounds, and building all of the apartments in one block means plenty of natural space left for everyone to enjoy. There’s definitely no shortage of space in the apartments either.

Our open plan living room with the bedrooms at the back, laundry to the left and cinema room to the right.

Shared resources within the complex means you don’t have to go far to get to the gym.

Higher living means a better view.

To make up for the lack of yard there is a balcony where you can dry your clothes (wind down rack shown above) and keep plants, as most Koreans do. The space even comes with a hose and a drain for this purpose.

The walkways are still tree lined despite the 13 buildings with a total of more than 500 apartments.

Our Korean style bed, until our sofa bed and then real bed arrive.

High rise buildings leave plenty of space for beautiful bike paths along the river and wetlands.

One of the big apartments blocks close to the river to be able to make the most of the bike / walking paths.
